Body structure
Members of the phylum Arthropoda are characterised by having divided and segmented bodies (Figure 1), a strong and hard exoskeleton and pairs of jointed appendages, which are structures, such as legs and antennae, that extend from the body wall.

Arthropods classification

Class Crustacea
The body: in most full-grown crustaceans, the body is formed of two regions, the cephalothorax and abdomen, it has a waxy cover that helps in preserving the body water and it is called chitinous cuticle.

Appendages: they are numerous and modified according to the function they perform. For example, some crustaceans use their legs to swim or walk, and others use hook-like legs to catch prey.

Eyes: their eyes are compound (consisting of a large number of lenses) and carried on the end of eyestalks extending from the head (Figure 2).

Respiration: most crustaceans are aquatic animals, but some others are adapted to life on land. They breathe using special structures called gills. This class includes shrimp, crab and lobster.

Figure (2)
Compound eyes in the crab.


Class Arachnida
The body: it is divided into two regions, the abdomen and the cephalothorax (fused head and thorax).

Appendages: they have six pairs of appendages: a pair of hooks, a pair of sensory legs used for chewing and squeezing food, and four pairs for walking (Figure 3).

Respiration: the majority breathe by two different mechanisms: the trachea and the book lungs, and some others use one mechanism only.

Eyes: simple front eyes that contain one lens are present in the head.

Sex: some are unisexual meaning that they have separate sexes. This class includes spiders and scorpions.

Figure (3)
An arachnid


Class Insecta:
Body: the body of insects consists of three main parts: the head, thorax and abdomen.

Appendages: they have three pairs that they use to walk, and they are characterised by having two pairs of wings, although one pair may be absent as in house flies, and some are wingless as with most ant species. They also have a pair of antennae.

Respiration: the tracheoles are used for breathing these are long tubes that extend from a spiracle in the exoskeleton throughout the body. Air enters through the spiracles, and oxygen passes into the blood by diffusion.

Eyes: they have a pair of compound eyes. This class includes mosquitoes, bees and locusts (Figure 4).

Figure (4)
An insect


Class Myriapoda
Body: a myriapod body consists of two main regions; the head and the trunk which is divided into multiple segments.

Appendages: the trunk segments carry many articulated (jointed) legs, their number may reach 200 pairs.

Respiration: they live on the land; so they breathe using the tracheoles, as in some arachnids and insects. This class includes scolopendra (Figure 5).
